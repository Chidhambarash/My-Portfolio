Why I Studied This Case

When I reviewed global procurement decisions made between 2022 and 2025, one pattern became impossible to ignore: many of the most confident sourcing strategies failed under real operational pressure.

The Collision

Old Ideas:

  • Supply chain sovereignty
  • Vertical integration
  • ESG-led sourcing

2025 Reality:

  • Rising cost of capital
  • Geopolitical instability
  • Execution gaps

I studied this period not as a historian, but as a buyer: to understand what assumptions broke, why they broke, and what actually worked.

The 2025 Reality Check

What changed in 2025 was not demand — it was tolerance for risk.

Across automotive, logistics, and technology, I observed the same shift: Strategy moved from visionary to verifiable. Buyers stopped funding ambition and started buying certainty. Liquidity, yield, and execution discipline overtook ideology.

Pattern 1

When Strategic Sourcing Failed

Northvolt and the battery sovereignty illusion

In the early 2020s, European OEMs pursued “battery sovereignty” to reduce dependence on Asian suppliers. Northvolt became the flagship, raising €14B+ with a strong ESG narrative. BMW’s €2B contract wasn't a buy—it was a strategic bet.

Pattern 2

When “Build” Was the Wrong Answer

Volkswagen, Cariad, and the make-vs-buy reckoning

Volkswagen attempted to insource its software future through Cariad. On paper, it looked like smart procurement logic: control the OS, own the data, keep the margins. In reality, it became a case study in sunk-cost blindness, accumulating €7.5B+ in losses and delaying key vehicle launches.

"Owning IP is meaningless if it doesn’t ship. Killing sunk cost is cheaper than extending failure."

Pattern 3

When Strategy Actually Worked

Maersk vs DSV: Asset ownership vs Control

Maersk (Vertical)

Owned ships, terminals, warehouses. Heavy fixed assets.

DSV (Asset-Light)

Carrier-agnostic, software-led, M&A driven. Controlled capacity without owning it.

DSV’s acquisition of DB Schenker created a freight giant that markets rewarded. Why? Because they passed agility to shippers. My takeaway: In volatile environments, control beats ownership. Prioritize partners who arbitrage complexity.

Why Defense Procurement Didn’t Break

While automotive and tech struggled, defense (e.g., Rheinmetall) remained stable. The difference was simple: Defense has a buyer of last resort (the State) underwriting the capital risk.

Critical sourcing insight: Commercial procurement cannot rely on political logic without commercial protection.
